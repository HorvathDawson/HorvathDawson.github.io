This post covers everything that changes when the Miata double wishbone is narrowed to fit the A40, focusing on decisions that must be locked in before the frame is welded. Spring rates, ARB diameter selection, damper setup, and other tunable parameters are in the companion tuning reference post.

All examples use 205mm tires, ET45, +70mm outer-to-outer (1435mm), giving 1230mm front track and 87.5mm narrowing per side. This nearly matches the stock A40 front track of 1232mm.

> Plots in this post are generated by [`scripts/plot_005b.py`](https://github.com/HorvathDawson/HorvathDawson.github.io/blob/main/scripts/plot_005b.py). All constants and formulas match the analysis below.

## What does NOT change

When the entire assembly translates as a unit on a custom frame, the following are preserved — but only if the inner pivot locations are correctly positioned at exactly 87.5mm inboard of their stock positions. The +/-1mm pickup tolerance in the fabrication section applies here:

- **Camber curve through travel.** Preserved. Corrective gain per degree of body roll scales by $T_{new}/T_{old}$ (each degree of roll produces less wheel travel on a narrower track).
- **Caster, KPI, mechanical trail.** Unchanged (properties of knuckle/arm orientations). Scan data gives KPI = 12.1° and caster = 0.0° (both ball joints share the same fore-aft offset of −8.8 mm from hub centre). These values are from a photogrammetric scan CAD model, not yet verified against the donor car.
- **Scrub radius from narrowing alone.** Horizontal narrowing preserves scrub radius (the KPI axis and contact patch translate together). However, changing tire diameter shifts the KPI ground intercept and changes scrub radius, even at stock ET45. See the scrub radius section below.
- **Anti-dive and anti-squat.** Side-view IC location is preserved. Anti-dive and anti-squat percentages change with the A40's longer wheelbase (2350mm vs 2265mm) and must be recalculated. Verify in SA V2.
- **Bump steer.** On a custom frame the rack mount moves with everything else. Caveat: if the rack narrows differently than the track, tie rod angles change and bump steer must be re-checked.

The local four-bar linkage geometry is identical to stock Miata. All relative arm angles, length ratios, and constraint equations are preserved. This is the whole point of option 2 (move pickup points inward rather than shorten arms).

## Geometry changes from narrowing

The frame is custom, so the suspension linkage (upper arm, lower arm, knuckle, hub, wheel) translates inboard as a unit. The four-bar linkage geometry in a local wheel-relative frame is identical to stock Miata.

What changes is how the suspension interacts with the vehicle as a whole.

### Roll center height

The roll center is found by drawing a line from each tire's contact patch to its front-view instant center (FVIC), then finding where those lines cross the vehicle centerline. When the entire assembly shifts inboard, both the contact patch and the FVIC move inward by the same amount. The roll center height scales linearly with the track ratio:

> About a 12.5% drop from narrowing. The tire height change adds only a few mm of RC shift on top of this. SA V2 captures both effects and should be used as the authoritative RC value.

### Roll stiffness

Roll stiffness from the springs is proportional to the square of the track width. When the body rolls by angle $\phi$, each wheel deflects vertically by $\frac{T}{2}\phi$, producing a restoring moment proportional to $T^2$:

$$K_\phi = \frac{K_w \cdot T^2}{2}$$

where $K_w$ is the wheel rate (N/m) at each corner.

> $$\frac{K_{\phi,new}}{K_{\phi,old}} = \left(\frac{1230}{1405}\right)^2 = 0.766$$
>
> About a 23% drop for the same wheel rate. Matching Miata roll stiffness with springs alone would need 30% stiffer wheel rates ($1/0.766 = 1.30$), which would hurt the ride. Anti-roll bars are the correct tool.

### Weight transfer

Lateral weight transfer is inversely proportional to track width:

$$\Delta W = \frac{m \cdot a_y \cdot h_{CG}}{T}$$

> At 0.8G, 950 kg, CG = 500 mm (held constant to isolate the track effect):
>
> Miata (1405mm): $\Delta W = \frac{950 \times 7.85 \times 0.50}{1.405} = 2{,}654 \text{ N}$
>
> A40 build (1230mm): $\Delta W = \frac{950 \times 7.85 \times 0.50}{1.230} = 3{,}032 \text{ N}$
>
> About 14% more from narrowing alone. With the A40's higher CG (~575mm vs ~460mm), total weight transfer is roughly 43% higher. This means higher peak loads on the outside tire, ball joints, and bushings, and roughly 1-3% less peak grip per axle from tire load sensitivity.

### Jacking force

$$\frac{F_{jack}}{F_{lat}} = \frac{h_{RC}}{T/2}$$

Since both the RC and the track scale by the same factor, this ratio is preserved. Jacking is not a concern from narrowing alone.

The current geometry puts the static RC at roughly 130mm for the A40 build. That sounds high, but the stock Miata computes to ~133mm with the same model, and the NA Miata is widely regarded as one of the best-handling affordable sports cars ever produced. The geometry works.

> At 1230mm track and ~130mm RC: jacking ratio = $130/615 = 0.211$, so 21% of the lateral tire force acts vertically. At 0.8G with 950kg: lateral force = $950 \times 7.85 = 7{,}458$ N, jacking force = $0.211 \times 7{,}458 \approx 1{,}572$ N. The stock Miata (1405mm track, ~133mm RC) has a jacking ratio of $133/702.5 = 0.189$, or 19%. The A40 build is slightly higher due to the narrower track, but the difference is small. The Miata handles this RC height without issue.
>
> The inner pivot height drops (LCA +25mm, UCA -15mm) are estimates that dominate the absolute RC value. These must be verified against the donor car. SA V2 is the authoritative tool once the donor measurements are in hand.

| | Donor Miata | A40 build | Ratio |

| RC.y sweep, full envelope | 283 mm | 185 mm | 0.65 |

#### Why static RC barely moved

The geometric identity is

$$RC_y = \frac{IC_y \cdot CP_x}{CP_x - IC_x} = \frac{IC_y \cdot CP_x}{\text{FVSA}_h}$$

Plugging in the donor gives 132.6 mm, the A40 gives 129.8 mm. This is a coincidence of fractional cancellation, not a structural property of the linkage. IC.y rose about 12 % from the taller tire raising the hub. CP.x dropped about 12.5 % from narrowing. FVSA stayed essentially constant because the linkage translates as a unit. The numerator change cancels the contact patch change. Narrowing without growing the tire would have dropped the RC about 12 %. Growing the tire without narrowing would have raised it about 12 %. We did both, and they cancelled.

#### Why the lateral migration shrinks 3x and the vertical only 1.5x

The dominant lever for migration is not track width itself, it is the ratio FVSA half length to half track. Donor sits at 2.23 and A40 sits at 2.55, a 14 % move. RC.x migration is highly nonlinear in this ratio: as it grows toward infinity you approach a parallel swing axle (zero lateral RC migration), as it shrinks toward 1 you approach a short swing axle (very large migration). At our operating point a 14 % move along that curve gives a 3x reduction in the lateral migration envelope. RC.y migration is closer to linear in the geometry, which is why the vertical envelope only drops by about a factor of 1.5 (185/283 = 0.65, close to the 0.875 track ratio).

We did not shrink migration by narrowing per se. We shrank it because narrowing combined with raising IC.y stretched the FVSA / half track ratio, pushing the geometry further toward the parallel swing axle end of the curve.

#### What this means for the car

The RC.y and RC.x components do different things, so it pays to split them.

| Quantity | Effect | Change on A40 |
|---|---|---|
| RC.y migration | jacking force and geometric vs elastic load transfer split | smaller envelope, steadier load transfer through curbs and combined inputs |
| RC.x migration | mid corner balance shift and yaw disturbance under asymmetric inputs (one wheel curb, asymmetric trail brake) | much smaller envelope, less wandering balance |

Roll gradient (degrees per g) goes the wrong way. The kinematic ceiling, the (heave, roll) corner where one wheel is at full bump and the other at full droop, does rise from 8.21 deg to 9.39 deg. That is a constraint, not a benefit. For the same shocks and springs, the chassis now rolls more degrees per g on a narrower track, since the same delta travel between left and right corresponds to a larger angle on a shorter half track. This is why the package needs an ARB rebalance: to put the operating roll gradient back where it was. Note also that ARB arm length scales with track, so a 12.5 % narrower track at the same torsion section is itself stiffer at the wheel, which the ARB sizing must account for.

Camber and scrub vs wheel travel are unchanged under a rigid translation of the inner pivots. The static operating point on those curves is preserved only if ride height is set so the linkage sits in the same pose. The plot script does exactly that, which is why the operating points match.

#### Caveats

- Front only. Steady state balance is set by the front to rear roll axis tilt, and only the front axle is analyzed here. The rear is a triangulated four link, covered in Post 006.
- Side view (anti dive, anti squat) is unchanged in geometry but should be re checked at the new ride.
- Static RC is sensitive to the pivot z assumptions (LCA pivot 25 mm above LBJ, UCA pivot 15 mm below UBJ). A 5 mm error there moves static RC about 10 mm and rescales the migration envelopes roughly proportionally. Verify on the donor before frame welding.
- The full envelope numbers above are worst case across the whole shock stroke. For normal driving inputs, the relevant quantity is the migration rate at the operating point, $dRC/d\phi$ and $dRC/dh$ near static, where the improvement is more modest.

#### Other operating cases

**Under braking.** Anti-dive percentage (set by the side-view IC) is unchanged. Narrowing doesn't affect side-view geometry.

**Under combined braking and cornering.** Dive compresses the front suspension, shifting FVICs and moving the front RC mid corner. Most noticeable on trail braking corner entry: the front RC drops below static, increasing body roll and shifting WT toward the elastic path. SA V2 can check the magnitude at expected dive displacements.

### Geometric vs elastic weight transfer

Lateral weight transfer travels through two paths: geometric (through the links, instant) and elastic (through springs/ARBs, lagging by the roll damping time constant). A lower RC shifts transfer into the elastic path, making turn-in character tunable via damper valving. The RC height choice in SA V2 should balance geometric/elastic split against jacking.

## Scrub radius

Scrub radius is the lateral distance at ground level between the steering axis (kingpin) ground intercept and the center of the tire contact patch. Positive scrub places the intercept inboard of the contact patch center. It governs self-centering under braking, steering feel, and kickback from road irregularities.

The steering axis passes through LBJ and UBJ on the knuckle. Their positions relative to the hub center are fixed by the casting and do not change with tire size, wheel offset, or narrowing. What changes is how far the axis must be projected downward to reach the ground plane.

### Why tire diameter matters

A taller tire raises the hub center. The KPI axis angle is fixed (set by the knuckle), so a higher hub means the axis intersects the ground further outboard. The contact patch center stays at the wheel centerline regardless of tire height. The net effect: scrub radius decreases with taller tires.

From the scan data:

- LBJ: 74.9 mm inboard, 93.6 mm below hub center
- UBJ: 124.7 mm inboard, 139.4 mm above hub center
- KPI = $\arctan\!\left(\frac{124.7 - 74.9}{139.4 + 93.6}\right) = \arctan\!\left(\frac{49.8}{233.0}\right) = 12.1°$

The change in scrub radius per mm of hub height change:

$$\Delta SR = -\Delta h \cdot \tan(\theta_{KPI})$$

For $\theta_{KPI} = 12.1°$: $\tan(12.1°) = 0.214$. Each additional mm of hub height reduces scrub radius by 0.214 mm.

### Tire comparison

| Tire | Free radius (mm) | $\Delta h$ from 185/60R14 | $\Delta SR$ (mm) |
|------|------------------:|------------------------:|-----------------:|
| 185/60R14 (stock Miata) | 288.5 | 0 | 0 |
| 185/70R15 | 320.0 | +31.5 | −6.7 |
| 195/65R15 | 317.3 | +28.8 | −6.2 |
| 205/65R15 | 323.8 | +35.3 | −7.6 |

Going from the stock 185/60R14 to a 205/65R15 reduces scrub radius by about 7.6 mm. That is a meaningful change, roughly equivalent to running a wheel with 8 mm less offset on the stock tire.

### Wheel offset effect

Changing wheel offset (ET) shifts the contact patch center by the offset difference. Scrub radius changes 1:1 with ET:

$$\Delta SR = \Delta ET$$

A lower-offset wheel (e.g. ET35 instead of ET45) moves the contact patch 10 mm outboard, increasing scrub radius by 10 mm. A higher-offset wheel does the opposite.

### Combined effect

The total scrub radius change from both tire size and offset:

$$\Delta SR_{total} = \Delta ET - \Delta h \cdot \tan(\theta_{KPI})$$

For this build, the tire diameter is increasing (reducing scrub) and the offset is TBD. If the goal is to maintain stock Miata scrub radius with a taller tire, the offset must decrease (lower ET) to compensate. For the 205/65R15 at stock ET45, scrub radius is about 7.6 mm less than the stock Miata baseline.

> Whether this matters in practice: a 5-7 mm scrub radius change is noticeable in steering feel under braking but not dangerous. Stock Miata scrub radius is small and positive (estimated 10-20 mm depending on exact hub-to-lug-face geometry). Reducing it by 7 mm keeps it positive and may slightly reduce steering kickback on rough surfaces. If it goes near zero or negative, braking stability improves but self-centering feel diminishes. SA V2 computes the exact value once the wheel and tire are finalized.

## Roll dynamics and anti-roll bars

The stock Miata front ARB won't fit the narrowed track — the bar's mounting geometry is tied to the factory subframe, not the track width per se, and a custom frame does not replicate those mounts. A custom bar is critical: the 23% spring roll stiffness drop plus higher CG means excessive roll without one. An ARB is a torsion spring connecting left and right suspension through end links. It only resists differential wheel motion (roll), not equal bump, adding roll stiffness without affecting ride compliance.

### Torsional stiffness

The torsional stiffness of a solid round bar:

$$K_{bar} = \frac{G \cdot \pi \cdot D^4}{32 \cdot L_{eff}}$$

where $G$ is the shear modulus (~80 GPa for steel), $D$ is the bar diameter, and $L_{eff}$ is the effective torsional length (the straight section between the pivot bushings).

The $D^4$ relationship means small diameter changes have a large effect: 22mm to 25mm increases torsional stiffness by 67% ($(25/22)^4 = 1.667$).

### ARB roll stiffness

The roll stiffness contribution depends on the bar's torsional stiffness and its installation ratio (how much the bar twists per radian of body roll):

$$K_{\phi,ARB} = K_{bar} \cdot IR_{bar}^2$$

The installation ratio $IR_{bar}$ is determined by:
- The bar arm length (pivot axis to end-link attachment on the bar)
- Where on the LCA the end link attaches (distance from LCA inner pivot)
- The LCA length and track width

For a simplified model where the end links attach at the ball joint end of the LCA:

$$IR_{bar} = \frac{T}{L_{arm}}$$

For a more realistic model where the end link attaches partway along the LCA at distance $r$ from the inner pivot:

$$IR_{bar} = \frac{r \cdot T}{L_{LCA} \cdot L_{arm}}$$

where $L_{LCA}$ is the full lower control arm length and $L_{arm}$ is the bar arm length. The exact installation ratio gets computed in SA V2.

The stock Miata front ARB (19mm — note that is the NA8 Sport/SE diameter; the base NA6 uses a 15mm bar, and the NA8 Sport/R package uses a 22mm bar) adds roughly 10,000 N-m/rad of front roll stiffness in conjunction with its specific installation geometry. This value is highly geometry-dependent (bar arm length and end-link attachment position on the LCA determine the installation ratio); the actual number for this build requires measuring the donor ARB geometry. Combined front and rear ARBs add roughly 23-60% on top of the spring roll stiffness, bringing roll from ~5 degrees down to ~3-4 degrees at 0.8G.

### Body roll: why ARBs are not optional

The steady-state roll angle:

$$\phi = \frac{m_s \cdot a_y \cdot (h_{CG} - h_{RC})}{K_{\phi,total} - m_s \cdot g \cdot (h_{CG} - h_{RC})}$$

The denominator includes a destabilizing gravity term that reduces the effective roll stiffness. At high CG heights this term is significant.

> **Miata baseline (0.8G, springs only, no ARB):**
>
> - $m_s \approx 820\text{kg}$ (sprung mass), $h_{CG} = 460\text{mm}$, $h_{RC} = 45\text{mm}$
> - Roll arm: $h_{CG} - h_{RC} = 415\text{mm}$
> - Front wheel rate: $K_w = 25{,}000 \times 0.63^2 = 9{,}920 \text{ N/m}$ (NA8 Sport/SE spring rate ~25 N/mm, MR = 0.63 from the [motion ratio calculation below](#motion-ratio); base NA6 spring is closer to 14-16 N/mm)
> - Front roll stiffness: $K_{\phi,f} = \frac{9{,}920 \times 1.405^2}{2} = 9{,}792 \text{ N}\!\cdot\!\text{m/rad}$
> - Rear ($K_s = 17\text{ N/mm}$, same MR = 0.63 assumed, T 1.42m): $K_{\phi,r} = \frac{17{,}000 \times 0.63^2 \times 1.42^2}{2} = 6{,}800 \text{ N}\!\cdot\!\text{m/rad}$
> - Total spring roll stiffness: $K_\phi = 16{,}592 \text{ N}\!\cdot\!\text{m/rad}$
> - Gravity term: $m_s g (h_{CG} - h_{RC}) = 820 \times 9.81 \times 0.415 = 3{,}340 \text{ N}\!\cdot\!\text{m/rad}$
> - Roll moment: $M = 820 \times 7.85 \times 0.415 = 2{,}671 \text{ N}\!\cdot\!\text{m}$
> - Roll angle: $\phi = \frac{2{,}671}{16{,}592 - 3{,}340} = 0.202 \text{ rad} = 11.5°$
>
> The stock Miata with factory ARBs rolls about 5° at 0.8G, so the ARBs are roughly doubling the total roll stiffness on top of the springs — they are not a trim, they are doing structural work.
>
> **A40 build (0.8G, same wheel rate, no ARB, 1230mm front track):**
>
> - $m_s \approx 850\text{kg}$, $h_{CG} = 575\text{mm}$ (estimated: Miata 460mm + ~115mm for the taller body and higher passenger position, +/-50mm uncertainty), $h_{RC} = 39\text{mm}$ (from 0.875 scaling)
> - Roll arm: $h_{CG} - h_{RC} = 536\text{mm}$
> - Front roll stiffness (same $K_w = 9{,}920$ N/m): $K_{\phi,f} = \frac{9{,}920 \times 1.230^2}{2} = 7{,}506 \text{ N}\!\cdot\!\text{m/rad}$
> - Rear (live axle, 20 N/mm spring, MR ~1.0, spring base ~950mm): $K_{\phi,r} = \frac{20{,}000 \times 0.950^2}{2} = 9{,}025 \text{ N}\!\cdot\!\text{m/rad}$
> - Total: $K_\phi = 16{,}531 \text{ N}\!\cdot\!\text{m/rad}$
> - Gravity term: $850 \times 9.81 \times 0.536 = 4{,}469 \text{ N}\!\cdot\!\text{m/rad}$
> - Roll moment: $850 \times 7.85 \times 0.536 = 3{,}576 \text{ N}\!\cdot\!\text{m}$
> - Roll angle: $\phi = \frac{3{,}576}{16{,}531 - 4{,}469} = 0.296 \text{ rad} = 17.0°$
>

> At ~17 degrees of body roll, the body tilt imposes about 17 degrees of positive camber on the outside tire. The corrective camber gain per degree of roll drops proportionally ($\times 1230/1405$): roughly 0.25-0.42 degrees per degree, giving about 4-7 degrees of correction. The outside tire ends up at roughly 10-13 degrees of positive camber, which destroys lateral grip. ARBs are not optional on this build.

Note: the roll formula uses sprung mass ($m_s$, excluding wheels/hubs/brakes). The sprung CG is slightly higher than the total-vehicle value used here, so roll arm is slightly underestimated. The A40 CG of 575mm has +/-50mm uncertainty.

Rear roll stiffness is estimated for a live axle: 20 N/mm spring, MR ~1.0, spring base ~950mm (coilovers near the bearing caps on a narrowed Ford 8-inch housing). If the coilovers mount further inboard, the lever arm and roll stiffness drop. The actual value depends on coilover mounting geometry set during frame design.

The rear roll center on a triangulated four-link sits much higher than the Miata IRS rear RC (~40-80mm). The two upper bars converge to a virtual intersection point typically 200-400mm above ground, depending on bar angles and mounting heights. This high RC pushes more rear lateral weight transfer through the geometric path, changing the front/rear roll couple distribution and LLTD. Once the four-link geometry work begins, Post 006 and SA V2 will determine the actual rear RC height.

The frame must include ARB mounting provisions (pivot bushing locations, end-link clearance) on both axles. Starting range is 22-25mm solid bar, but do not finalize diameter until the CG is measured on the rolling chassis (tilt test or pendulum test). ARB sizing, bar fabrication, spring rates, damper setup, and balance tuning are covered in the tuning reference post.

## Springs, damping, and motion ratio

### Motion ratio

The motion ratio (MR) is the ratio of spring/damper displacement to wheel displacement. For a coilover on the lower control arm (see [ISMA Tech-01 *Motion Ratios*, p. 5](/assets/projects/a40-austin/blog/Tech-01-Motion-ratios-4.pdf#page=5) ([source](https://ismasupers.com/wp-content/uploads/2021/11/Tech-01-Motion-ratios-4.pdf)) for the geometric construction):

$$MR = \frac{D_{spring}}{D_{arm}} \cdot \sin(\alpha)$$

All three quantities are taken in the plane perpendicular to the LCA hinge axis: $D_{spring}$ is the distance from that axis to the coilover lower mount, $D_{arm}$ is the distance from the same axis to the wheel centerline (at half-track), and $\alpha$ is the angle between the coilover centerline and the LCA at the lower mount. Wheel rate and effective damping scale with $MR^2$, so small changes in $\alpha$ matter.

> **Stock Miata front:** Lower arm ~360 mm to the LBJ, coilover lower mount ~290 mm out (BJ-referenced lever 290/360 ≈ 0.81). Coilover angle ~80°. The often-cited "Miata MR ≈ 0.80" comes from this BJ-referenced calculation, but for the $K_\phi = K_w \, T^2/2$ formula used elsewhere in the post, MR must be referenced to the same point as $T$ — the wheel centerline. Using the wheel-centerline lever from the donor scan ($240/374.5 = 0.641$) and $\sin 80° = 0.985$ gives $MR = 0.63$. This is the value used everywhere else in this post for Miata roll/spring math.
>
> **A40 build (donor LCA reused):** Same arm on a custom frame, so the lever ratio is fixed by the donor dimensions. With both legs measured perpendicular to the pivot axis, $D_{arm} = 702.5 - 328 = 374.5$ mm (half-track minus the LCA pivot's distance from car centreline) and $D_{spring} = 240$ mm (donor scan), giving a lever ratio of $240/374.5 = 0.641$. Only $\alpha$ — set by the shock-tower position on the custom frame — is still free.

The shock tower must fit between the frame rails, clear the engine, and carry ~5,000 N in bump. This is a packaging constraint set during frame design, not an afterthought.

### Coilover stroke

The shock moves less than the wheel by the motion ratio. With 70-75mm bump and 85-90mm droop at the wheel, the required shock stroke is wheel travel times MR. At MR = 0.63: ~44-47mm compression stroke and ~54-57mm extension stroke, roughly 98-104mm total shock stroke. Units like the Koni 8610 series and Bilstein SPS are motorsport/custom-order dampers and must be specified with the required stroke at time of order — they do not ship in a single standard length. Confirm the chosen unit's stroke accommodates the actual travel at the final MR before committing to a shock tower position.

Spring rate selection, ride frequency targets, and damper valving are covered in the tuning reference post.

## Suspension travel

The suspension linkage translates as a unit with the taller tire, so bump and droop travel are preserved from the Miata donor (roughly 70-75mm bump, 85-90mm droop).

The travel budget is tight because of body roll. At 3.5 degrees of roll on a 1230mm track, the outside wheel deflects ~38mm into bump. A 20mm road input at speed amplifies to 26-30mm (DAF 1.3-1.5x, assuming a damping ratio $\zeta \approx 0.3$-$0.4$). Combined: 64-68mm, leaving almost no margin from 70-75mm total. Progressive bump stops must match the expected combined roll + road displacement. If the front hits the stop before the rear (or vice versa), the handling balance shifts abruptly mid-corner.

## Structural loads and compliance

Control arm forces scale with the ~43% higher weight transfer from narrowing plus the higher CG. Frame brackets need turned steel or DOM tube inserts for load distribution. Gusset upper arm mounts and triangulate into the frame tubes. Inspect wheel bearing preload at shakedown and regular intervals.

With rubber bushings (correct for a street car), the higher lateral loads cause more bushing deflection, and the different tie rod angles change the deflection-to-toe relationship. Polyurethane bushings reduce compliance effects.

## Fitment, fabrication, and alignment

### Clearance checks

- **Lower arm vs frame rail.** Mock up at full bump AND full lock with actual arms on the tacked frame.
- **Tire to frame at full lock.** Budget for losing 12-18 degrees of lock per side from stock Miata — this is a body clearance constraint (fender opening and inner wheel arch limit how far the tire can swing), not a rack capacity limit. A correctly narrowed rack does not reduce maximum rack travel. Turning radius roughly doubles (~9.4m to ~16-20m). The actual lock limit depends on the A40's fender geometry and must be verified at full lock with the actual arms on the tacked frame.
- **Exhaust routing.** Lower arms now occupy the exhaust path. Custom headers required. Plan before finalizing pickup locations.
- **Steering shaft.** May need a second U-joint or different intermediate shaft length. Check binding at full lock.
- **Rack-to-engine clearance.** At 175mm total narrowing, the housing sits closer to the engine. Packaging constraint during frame design.
- **Coilover-to-frame rail.** Check spring body clearance at full bump with actual spring diameter. Notch and gusset the rail if needed.
- **Brake lines.** All hard and flex lines will be custom.
- **Ball joint life.** The higher outside ball joint loads mean stock NA joints are adequate for street with annual inspection. For track: upgraded rebuildable joints (QA1, FK, Aurora, Howe).

### Tolerances

Suspension pickup locations: +/-1mm. Upper arm inner mounts: +/-0.5mm. Left-to-right symmetry matters more than absolute position (asymmetry causes pull that alignment can't correct).

Process: tack all mounts, bolt in actual control arms and knuckles on the jig, measure from centerline to each pickup, verify left-to-right symmetry before full welding. A straight bar through both hub bores with a digital level catches camber errors side-to-side. Caster must be verified separately by measuring the ball joint axis angle in side view (fore-aft offset between upper and lower ball joint centers at a known ride height).

### Alignment provisions

The frame needs built-in adjustment:

- **Camber:** Shims or eccentric cam bolts at the upper arm inboard mounts. Budget +/-2 degrees.
- **Caster:** Slotted holes on the upper mount fore-aft. Target 5-7 degrees positive, budget +/-2.
- **Toe:** Threaded tie rod ends. No special frame provision needed.
- **Bump steer:** Rack mount needs vertical adjustment. Design for at least +/-15mm of slotted height adjustment (shim plates or slotted mount). 8mm is the minimum viable range; the extra margin costs nothing in fabrication and prevents being locked out of a geometry correction if the tacked rack height needs significant correction.

## Design iteration with SA V2

SA V2 models the full 3D geometry in the context of the A40, not to match Miata stock values. The Miata geometry was optimized for ~460mm CG, ~1405mm front track, and ~960kg. The A40 has a CG over 100mm higher, a track around 1230mm, and different weight distribution. The optimal roll center, spring rates, and roll stiffness distribution are different.

Workflow:
1. Enter stock MX-5 NA geometry as baseline
2. Set the A40 wheelbase (2350mm), estimated weight distribution, and COG height
3. Move inner pickup points inward to the target track width
4. Check roll center height and migration. Target an RC height for this car's COG and track. Stay below ~80-90mm to avoid jacking.
5. Verify camber curve through full travel
6. Set the shock/spring upper mount location to give a usable motion ratio
7. Verify anti-dive and anti-squat percentages for the A40 wheelbase
8. Check Ackermann geometry with the planned rack narrowing
9. Design the ARB rates to fill the gap between spring roll stiffness and total roll stiffness needed

For the rationale behind each check (why the RC drops, why roll stiffness falls, what to look for), see the sections above. Screenshots and actual analysis results are in the SA V2 results post.
