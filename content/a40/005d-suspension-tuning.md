This post covers the parameters that can be changed after the frame is built: spring rates, ARB sizing, damper valving, and alignment targets. Everything here assumes the geometry decisions from the geometry design reference post are finalized, the frame is welded, and the shock tower position is set.

> Plots in this post are generated by [`scripts/plot_005b.py`](https://github.com/HorvathDawson/HorvathDawson.github.io/blob/main/scripts/plot_005b.py). All constants and formulas match the geometry design reference.

## Spring rate and ride frequency

### Corner mass

Total vehicle mass minus unsprung mass gives the sprung mass. Unsprung mass is everything below the spring: wheel, tire, hub, brake rotor, caliper, and roughly half the control arm weight (the other half is carried by the body through the spring). For a Miata-based corner, typical unsprung mass is 25-30 kg per corner.

| Component | Approximate mass |
|-----------|-----------------|
| Wheel + tire (14" steel or 15" alloy) | 14-18 kg |
| Hub + bearing + knuckle | 4-6 kg |
| Brake rotor + caliper | 4-6 kg |
| Half of upper + lower arm | 2-4 kg |
| **Total unsprung per corner** | **25-30 kg** |

Sprung mass = total mass - (4 x unsprung per corner). For the A40 at ~950 kg total: $m_s \approx 950 - 4 \times 27 \approx 840\text{-}850$ kg.

Corner mass = sprung mass x axle weight fraction / 2. With the engine further forward than a Miata, estimate ~55/45 F/R for the A40. The front corner mass is $850 \times 0.55 / 2 \approx 234$ kg. Rear corner: $850 \times 0.45 / 2 \approx 191$ kg.

To measure instead of estimate: put the car on four corner scales. Each scale reads the total corner mass (sprung + unsprung). Subtract the unsprung mass (weigh a complete hub/brake/wheel assembly separately) to get the sprung corner mass.

### Ride frequency

The undamped natural frequency at each corner is:

$$f_n = \frac{1}{2\pi}\sqrt{\frac{K_w}{m_c}}$$

where $K_w$ is the wheel rate ($K_s \cdot MR^2$) and $m_c$ is the sprung corner mass.

Target 1.2-1.6 Hz for a street car. Below 1.0 Hz feels floaty; above 2.0 Hz is harsh. Front and rear frequencies should be within ~0.2 Hz, with the **rear frequency equal to or slightly above front** (0.0-0.2 Hz higher). A front frequency higher than rear makes the car pitch nose-down over road crests and recover slowly.

## ARB sizing

Without ARBs the A40 rolls ~10.3 degrees at 0.8G, destroying grip (see the geometry design reference for the full derivation). The springs alone provide ~24,300 N-m/rad of roll stiffness, but the target is 55,000-72,000 N-m/rad to limit roll to 3-4 degrees. The front and rear ARBs together need to add roughly 31,000-48,000 N-m/rad.

Bar torsional stiffness scales with $D^4$ (formula and installation ratio derivation in the geometry design reference). The plot below maps required total roll stiffness to bar diameter.

### LLTD and balance

The front/rear ARB split sets the lateral load transfer distribution (LLTD): $LLTD = \Delta W_f / (\Delta W_f + \Delta W_r)$. LLTD determines which axle saturates first (understeer vs oversteer). Target 1-3% above the front weight fraction for mild understeer. A higher CG amplifies sensitivity to LLTD: a single ARB adjustment hole can change the balance noticeably.

Starting range: 22-25mm solid for ~950kg. Stock Miata front is 15mm (base NA6) or 19mm (NA8 Sport/SE) or 22mm (NA8 Sport/R package). Budget for mounting provisions on both axles; the rear bar itself is conditional on testing once the car is on its wheels (many live-axle four-link builds run no rear ARB initially and add one only if roll balance testing shows it is needed — see the four-link post). The exact bar diameters and arm lengths get set after SA V2 gives the target front/rear roll stiffness split.

### Making the bar

Options: bend from round bar stock (4130 or 1018/1020 mild steel; 4130 needs stress relief after bending), use a universal adjustable bar kit (Speedway Motors etc.), or modify a Miata bar (cheapest but limits diameter choice). Use adjustable end links (rod end + threaded rod) to avoid binding at full travel or free play before engagement.

## Damper setup

Adjustable dampers are worth the cost since damping requirements won't be known until the shock tower location is finalized and the car is on its wheels. The A40 has higher roll inertia and lower spring roll stiffness than a Miata. Target roll damping ratio 0.4-0.5 (below 0.3 the car oscillates in roll, causing grip pulsing). Stiffer rebound damping raises the ratio but risks packing down over successive bumps. The rebound/compression split must be designed for this car's roll inertia, not carried over from Miata valving.

## Static camber and alignment targets

With ARBs limiting roll to 3-3.5 degrees, the outside tire still picks up 3-3.5 degrees of positive camber from body roll minus the corrective gain (~0.25-0.42 degrees per degree of roll, so 0.9-1.5 degrees of correction). The outside tire ends up at roughly 1.7-2.6 degrees positive relative to static. (The lower bound comes from maximum gain 0.42 deg/deg at minimum roll 3.0°: 3.0 - 1.26 = 1.74°. A 1.5° result would require a gain of 0.50 deg/deg, which exceeds the A40's corrective gain range.) To keep the outside tire near zero camber at the limit, set static camber to -2.5 to -3.0 degrees. This is aggressive for a street car (inner tire wear), so it becomes a tuning decision: -1.5 to -2.0 for street driving, -2.5 to -3.0 for mixed or track use.

### Alignment ranges

- **Camber:** -1.5 to -2.0 deg for street, -2.5 to -3.0 deg for track/autocross.
- **Caster:** 5-7 degrees positive. More caster adds straight-line stability and camber gain in corners at the cost of heavier steering (less of a concern with EPAS).
- **Toe:** Near zero front toe for a street car. Slight front toe-out (1-2mm total) sharpens initial turn-in response for autocross but can feel twitchy on the highway.

### Rear axle squaring

For the live rear axle on the four-link, rear toe is not individually adjustable in the same way as an IRS. The axle must be centered and squared perpendicular to the vehicle centerline during assembly. Two distinct failure modes apply:

- **Lateral offset (2-3mm off-center, axle still perpendicular):** Creates unequal track width and asymmetric driveshaft angles left-to-right. The car feels different in left vs right corners. Does not create a straight-line pull.
- **Angular skew (0.1-0.2° from perpendicular):** Acts as a fixed rear steer input on both wheels in the same direction. At 2350mm wheelbase, 0.1° of skew corresponds to ~4mm of tracking difference, which drivers feel as a persistent pull that front toe adjustment cannot correct.

Verify axle centering from centerline to each hub flange face, and verify squaring by checking diagonal measurements from a common reference point, before tacking the four-link brackets. Re-check both after final weld (brackets pull during welding).
